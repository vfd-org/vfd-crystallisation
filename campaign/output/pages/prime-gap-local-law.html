Prime-gap abundances from an all-places product (and twins to 0.003%)

The relative abundance of prime pairs at every even gap h is the product of (p−1)/(p−2) over the odd primes dividing h — gap-6 pairs are exactly twice as common as twins, gap-210 pairs 16/5 as common — and the absolute twin count to 5×10⁷ matches the Hardy–Littlewood all-places integral to 0.003%. Classical mathematics (HL 1923), verified live as the first rows of a prime-phenomena ledger run on the closure-diffraction instrument.

Everything that goes in

  1. A prime sieve to 5×10⁷ (nothing else — no fitting surface exists)
  2. Per-place admissibility counting: each odd prime p dividing the gap contributes (p−1)/(p−2)
  3. Hardy–Littlewood constant C2 as the convergent all-places product
  4. Theorem anchors run alongside: Dirichlet equidistribution + Q(√5) splitting, both PASS

How to kill this claim

Run python3 -m lab.prime_ledger with a larger X: any gap whose abundance drifts from its local product, or a twin count diverging from the HL integral, kills the row. Every gate and artifact is in out/prime_ledger.json.
