# How to Read This Book

Before we begin, a short map of the journey and a key to the way I make claims, so that you always know where you stand.

**The one idea.** This book makes a single proposal and then tests it. Take one maximally constrained geometric structure, a four-dimensional crystal called the 600-cell, and ask whether the things physics normally assumes, the number of dimensions of space, the directions you can move in, the flow of time, the fields, and some of the constants of nature, can be *read off* that structure rather than put in by hand. The first half of the book builds the geometric core. The second half asks what that core actually delivers: what is genuinely derived, what is only suggestive, and what remains open. That is the whole arc.

**The two columns.** I am an advocate for this idea, not a neutral reporter, and I have tried to earn your trust by keeping two things apart on every page. There are claims you can *check*, facts about a definite mathematical object that you, or a computer, can confirm or refute, and there are *interpretations*, the steps where I read physical meaning into those facts. The checkable claims stand whether or not the framework is right about the world. The interpretations are the bet. Whenever the book leans on the bet, I say so in plain words.

**How to read a bold sentence.** This book contains lines like "the crystal knows the dimension of space" and "the crystal forces time to run in waves." They are deliberate, and I mean them, but each one has three layers, and the whole art of reading this book is keeping them apart:

- *Mathematical fact* — the 600-cell genuinely has the property described. This is settled and not in dispute.
- *Framework identification* — the proposal reads that property as physically meaningful, as telling us something true about the real world.
- *Open risk* — that identification may be wrong, and nothing in the mathematics compels you to accept it.

When you meet a striking claim, ask which layer it lives in. I have tried to mark the boundary every time; this is how to hold the book at the right distance, neither dismissing the facts nor swallowing the interpretation whole.

**The grades.** Near the end you will find an Honest Ledger that grades every checkable claim from strongest to weakest. It is worth learning the words now, because the whole book speaks in them:

- **Theorem** — proven and machine-checked, exact.
- **Derived** — established at the working level a physicist trusts, with the error stated and one point of strict mathematical rigor still owed.
- **Conditional** — derived under a single clearly named assumption.
- **Witnessed** — a number that lands on a measured value, fitted rather than fully derived.
- **Tie** — a fixed prediction laid against data that cannot yet prefer or reject it.
- **Open** — named honestly, not derived.

Hold these in mind and the book will never ask you to take more on trust than it has earned. With the map drawn and the key in hand, let us go inside.
