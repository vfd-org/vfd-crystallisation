\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Cortical oscillations and travelling waves carry working-memory content and reorganise systematically under anaesthesia~\cite{lundqvist2016gamma,davis2020spontaneous,stoll2025convergent}. Standard scalar measures --- band power, pairwise phase-locking value (PLV), global synchrony --- conflate distinct wave-organisation regimes. We define a $9$-dimensional per-window closure feature vector over multi-band cortical phase graphs (theta, alpha, beta, gamma) including local smoothness, global Kuramoto order and its temporal variability, task-subnet order, $\alpha$- and $\beta$-band phase-gradient directionality (PGD), $\theta$--$\gamma$ phase-amplitude coupling, $\alpha$:$\beta$ cross-frequency PLV, and a saturating surrogate-deviation. Applied to the OpenNeuro \texttt{ds005620} propofol-sedation EEG corpus (eyes-closed awake vs propofol-sedated rest, \SI{240}{\second} per condition, \num{64}-channel BrainVision \SI{5}{\kilo\hertz} resampled to \SI{250}{\hertz}; $n=14$ pilot~$+$~held-out cohort), a per-subject calibrated displacement test --- distance from each subject's awake feature-vector mean in standardised feature space --- separates the sedated condition from awake in $14/14$ subjects, paired $t=7.11$, $p=8.0\!\times\!10^{-6}$; Wilcoxon $W=105$, $p=6.1\!\times\!10^{-5}$; Cohen's paired $d_z=1.90$; permutation $p<2\!\times\!10^{-4}$ ($0/5000$ within-subject shuffles matched the observed mean). The result holds under leave-one-subject-out re-standardisation ($14/14$, paired $t=7.00$, $d_z=1.87$). The displacement decomposes into four Benjamini--Hochberg-corrected per-feature effects: per-channel $\alpha$:$\beta$ cross-band PLV increases ($14/14$, $q=7\!\times\!10^{-6}$), $\beta$-band PGD increases ($13/14$, $q=0.007$), global Kuramoto order increases ($11/14$, $q=0.032$), and $\theta$--$\gamma$ phase-amplitude coupling decreases ($11/14$, $q=0.032$). Spatially, the $\beta$-PGD increase is whole-cortex (every region $q<0.005$); $\alpha$-PGD increase is frontal-selective ($13/14$, $q=0.0024$), consistent with the well-known propofol frontal-alpha pattern~\cite{purdon2013electroencephalogram,ching2010thalamocortical}, with the textbook posterior decrease being idiosyncratic across subjects rather than population-consistent. Per-channel-aggregated cross-band $\alpha$:$\beta$ PLV is broadly elevated under sedation: ratios $1$:$1$ through $2.5$:$1$ all show $14/14$ subjects with increase ($q<4\!\times\!10^{-6}$), $3$:$1$ in $12/14$ ($q=0.0014$); $4$:$1$ not significant. The displacement survives partialling out classical band-power, mean PLV, and global synchrony baselines, and is robust to leave-one-subject-out re-standardisation. \textbf{Source-localised replication} on \texttt{fsaverage} cortical anatomy with sLORETA inverse and a $68$-ROI Desikan--Killiany parcellation recovers the same displacement at the source level with a \emph{larger} effect size ($14/14$, $t=9.72$, $p=2.5\!\times\!10^{-7}$, $d_z=2.60$); the two strongest sensor-space effects (cross-band PLV, $\beta$-PGD) reach $q<10^{-6}$ at the source level, arguing against scalp-electrode volume conduction as the sole explanation (source leakage and template-anatomy effects remain; the source pipeline uses an ad-hoc identity noise covariance with no empty-room data). \textbf{Cross-cohort replication} on an independent clinical-anaesthesia EEG dataset (OpenNeuro \texttt{ds004541}, $n=7$ patients undergoing general anaesthesia, mixed-agent regime) reproduces the displacement at comparable in-sample effect size ($7/7$, $t=5.25$, $p=1.9\!\times\!10^{-3}$, $d_z=1.98$); the cross-validated LOSO estimate on this cohort is smaller ($d_z = 1.16$ at $n=7$, Jaccard $= 0.52$) and is the honest cross-fold number, reported separately in the cross-substrate companion~\citep{SmartClosureDistance}; the $\theta$--$\gamma$ PAC sign does not reverse, so the corresponding pre-stated falsification criterion is not triggered. The pattern is internally coherent: propofol increases wave-coherence at global and cross-band-locking levels while decreasing a putative cortical-coupling signature ($\theta$--$\gamma$ PAC). We release the full pipeline, per-subject CSVs, and falsification criteria as an open artifact.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\noindent\emph{Scope note.} This note does not claim to detect consciousness directly. It identifies a reproducible, multi-axis cortical wave-organisation displacement between awake and anaesthetised states. The consciousness interpretation is handled only in Paper III (\emph{From Processing to Point of View}) and remains conditional on the Access Principle (P-A).

Cortical computation is increasingly described as organised by travelling waves and burst-like phase structure~\cite{miller2018working,lundqvist2016gamma,bastos2020laminar,davis2020spontaneous,muller2018cortical,zhang2018theta,alamia2019alpha}, and anaesthetics produce convergent changes in cortical phase alignment despite acting through different molecular targets~\cite{stoll2025convergent,purdon2013electroencephalogram,ching2010thalamocortical,lewis2012rapid}. Standard EEG-derived measures --- band power, pairwise PLV, mean Kuramoto order --- compress this multi-dimensional structure to scalars that confuse rigid drug-induced phase locking with flexible task-coupled coherence. The empirical question this note addresses is narrow and concrete: \emph{is there a single feature space in which propofol sedation produces a structured, FDR-corrected, replicated displacement of cortical state, and what is the content of that displacement?}

We define such a feature space directly: a $9$-dimensional per-window closure feature vector over multi-band cortical phase graphs. The vector includes local phase smoothness across electrode adjacency, the Kuramoto order parameter on the alpha band and its within-trial temporal variability, the order parameter on a frontal task subnetwork (since propofol's signature is frontal), beta- and alpha-band phase-gradient directionality (PGD; a measure of travelling-wave coherence in space), $\theta$--$\gamma$ phase-amplitude coupling on the frontal subnetwork (a putative information-transfer signature~\cite{tort2010measuring}), $\alpha$:$\beta$ $n$:$m$ cross-frequency PLV, and a saturating $z$-distance from a phase-shuffled surrogate distribution. A separate residual-distance metric is defined as a weighted Euclidean distance from a target-state template; we show below that on real propofol data the synthetic-anchored template direction is partially incorrect, motivating a per-subject calibrated displacement test as the inferentially appropriate measure.

The contribution is empirical, not theoretical. We release the implementation as an open Apache-2.0 / CC~BY~4.0 artifact and publish full per-window CSVs, falsification criteria, and a permutation-validated displacement statistic. The rest of this note describes the pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:methods}), the n=14 result (Section~\ref{sec:results}), the spatial decomposition recovering frontal-alpha anteriorisation (Section~\ref{sec:spatial}), and limitations (Section~\ref{sec:limits}).

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Dataset}

OpenNeuro \texttt{ds005620}~\cite{bajwa2024dataset}: $\num{64}$-channel BrainVision EEG (\SI{5}{\kilo\hertz}), eyes-closed awake (\texttt{task-awake\_acq-EC}) versus propofol-sedated rest (\texttt{task-sed\_acq-rest\_run-1}), recorded at the University of Oslo. Per-subject nominal duration is $\SI{5}{\minute}$ per condition; the analysis uses up to $\SI{240}{\second}$ post-burnin per condition. Sub-$1017$ has a shorter $\SI{146}{\second}$ sed recording, retained as-is (yielding $\sim\!282$ vs $469$ windows in the standard cases). Pilot cohort: subjects $\{1010,1016,1022,1033,1036,1045,1046,1055\}$ ($n=8$). Held-out replication cohort: $\{1017,1024,1057,1062,1064,1074\}$ ($n=6$). Combined $n=14$. Subject \num{1037} is marked excluded in the dataset's \texttt{participants.tsv} and is not used.

\subsection{Preprocessing}

For each condition file we (i)~load the BrainVision triplet (.vhdr/.eeg/.vmrk) via MNE-Python~\cite{gramfort2014mne}; (ii)~drop non-EEG physiological channels (EOG/EMG); (iii)~apply the standard \texttt{standard\_1005} montage and discard channels without a $3$-D position; (iv)~resample from $\SI{5}{\kilo\hertz}$ to $\SI{250}{\hertz}$; (v)~band-pass-filter $\num{4}$--$\SI{45}{\hertz}$. The first $\SI{5}{\second}$ of each recording are discarded as a burn-in; up to $\SI{240}{\second}$ of post-burnin data are used per condition.

Per-band analytic signals are computed by MNE-Python's bandpass filter followed by Hilbert transform, in four bands: theta ($4$--$\SI{8}{\hertz}$), alpha ($8$--$\SI{13}{\hertz}$), beta ($15$--$\SI{30}{\hertz}$), gamma ($30$--$\SI{45}{\hertz}$). Electrode coordinates are projected from $\mathbb{R}^3$ to $\mathbb{R}^2$ via stereographic-style flattening, and a $k$-nearest-neighbours adjacency ($k=8$) is constructed in the projected coordinates. The frontal task subnetwork is fixed to the $14$ electrodes \{Fp1,Fpz,Fp2,AF3,AFz,AF4,F3,F1,Fz,F2,F4,FC1,FCz,FC2\} when present in the recording.

\subsection{Closure feature vector}

For each $1$\,s sliding window with $0.5$\,s step (50\% overlap), the per-window closure vector $\mathbf{f}\in\mathbb{R}^9$ is computed as follows.

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{local\_smoothness}: $\langle\cos(\phi^{\alpha}_i-\phi^{\alpha}_j)\rangle_{(i,j)\in E}$ averaged over the window, where $E$ is the adjacency edge set and $\phi^{\alpha}$ is alpha-band analytic phase.
\item \textbf{global\_order}: time-mean of $R_{\alpha}(t)=|\langle e^{i\phi^{\alpha}_n(t)}\rangle_n|$, the Kuramoto order parameter on alpha~\cite{kuramoto1984chemical}.
\item \textbf{global\_order\_std}: temporal standard deviation of $R_\alpha(t)$ within the window, a standard metastability index in coupled-oscillator descriptions of cortex~\cite{deco2016metastability}.
\item \textbf{task\_order}: time-mean of $R_{\beta}^{S}(t)=|\langle e^{i\phi^{\beta}_n(t)}\rangle_{n\in S}|$ on the frontal subnetwork $S$.
\item \textbf{pgd\_alpha} / \textbf{pgd\_beta}: phase-gradient directionality~\cite{rubino2006propagating,muller2018cortical}. Per-node $\mathbb{R}^2$ phase gradients are obtained via local least-squares fits using complex-valued differences $\arg(z_j z_i^*)$ over neighbours (handling phase wrapping). PGD is the directional consistency $|\langle\nabla\phi\rangle|/\langle|\nabla\phi|\rangle$ over (time-subsampled, factor 5) nodes and time. Range: $[0,1]$ ($1$ = stable plane wave; $0$ = isotropic / locked-flat).
\item \textbf{pac\_theta\_gamma}: mean-resultant-length phase-amplitude coupling on the frontal subnetwork, $|\langle A^{\gamma}_n(t)\,e^{i\phi^{\theta}_n(t)}\rangle_{n,t}|/\langle A^{\gamma}_n(t)\rangle_{n,t}$. The mean-resultant-length form was introduced by Canolty et al.~\cite{canolty2006high}; relative to bin-based modulation indices~\cite{tort2010measuring}, this formulation avoids finite-sample bias from sparse phase bins.
\item \textbf{cross\_band\_plv\_alpha\_beta}: $n$:$m$ phase-locking~\cite{tass1998detection} computed per-channel and aggregated. For each channel $n$, the within-window per-channel PLV is $\rho_n = |\langle e^{i(\phi^{\alpha}_n(t)-r\phi^{\beta}_n(t))}\rangle_t|$; the feature is $\langle\rho_n\rangle_n$. Crucially, phases are circular variables: arithmetic-mean-of-phases would be ill-defined and produce a similarity-of-phase-distributions surrogate rather than n:m PLV. We therefore aggregate at the PLV level, not the phase level. We sweep $r\in\{1,1.5,2,2.5,3,4\}$ in Section~\ref{sec:nm}.
\item \textbf{surrogate\_z}: $\tanh\!\big((|z_{\text{smooth}}|+|z_{\text{order}}|)/(2\cdot 5)\big)$, where $z_{\bullet}$ are $z$-scores against a per-node circularly-shifted surrogate ensemble. The saturating transform is bounded in $[0,1]$.
\end{enumerate}

A baseline feature vector $\mathbf{b}\in\mathbb{R}^7$ is computed per window for comparison: per-band mean squared analytic amplitudes (alpha, beta, gamma; band-power proxies), mean random-pair PLV on alpha and on beta, and global synchrony $|\langle e^{i\phi}\rangle_n|$ on alpha and on beta.

\subsection{Per-subject calibrated displacement test}

We apply two displacement tests:

\paragraph{Synthetic-anchored basin distance.}
Two reference templates --- \textsc{reportable} (flexible-task-coupled basin) and \textsc{anesthetic} (rigid-locking basin) --- are anchored from synthetic-trial empirical means and used to compute weighted Euclidean residuals $R_{\text{rep}}(t)$ and $R_{\text{an}}(t)$ per window. This test is reported for completeness; on real propofol it does not strongly capture the empirical regime (Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\paragraph{Per-subject calibrated drift.}
The full feature matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times 9}$ over all windows of all subjects is column-standardised to $Z$ \emph{using awake-condition windows only}: $\mu = \mathrm{mean}\{X_w : \text{awake}\}$, $\sigma = \mathrm{std}\{X_w : \text{awake}\}$, $Z = (X-\mu)/\sigma$. Standardising on awake windows alone avoids the circularity of pre-compressing across-condition variance before measuring the displacement. For each subject $s$, the personal awake reference is $\bar{z}_s = \mathrm{mean}\{z_w : w\in s, \text{awake}\}$. Per-window drift is $d_w = \|z_w - \bar{z}_{\text{subject}(w)}\|_2$. Per-condition drift means are compared between subjects via paired $t$ and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, with paired Cohen's $d_z = \overline{\Delta}/\mathrm{SD}(\Delta)$ as the effect-size measure. As an independent robustness check, a leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) variant recomputes $\mu,\sigma$ from the remaining $13$ subjects' awake windows for each held-out subject's test, ensuring no within-subject information enters the standardisation. The 5000-shuffle permutation null is constructed by independently permuting awake/sed labels within each subject's windows.

\subsection{Per-feature decomposition with FDR correction}

For each closure feature $f_k$ ($k=1,\dots,9$), per-subject $\Delta_s = \bar{f}_k^{\text{awake}} - \bar{f}_k^{\text{sed}}$ deltas are computed, and a paired $t$-test is run across subjects. $p$-values are corrected for multiple comparisons across the $9$ features using the Benjamini--Hochberg false-discovery-rate procedure~\cite{benjamini1995controlling} with $q=0.05$.

\subsection{Spatial localisation and $n$:$m$ specificity}

For spatial localisation, we partition electrodes into four anatomical regions (frontal, central, parietal, occipital) and compute regional $\mathrm{PGD}_\alpha$ and $\mathrm{PGD}_\beta$ on the per-region subset of channels. For $n$:$m$ specificity, we sweep $r\in\{1,1.5,2,2.5,3,4\}$ in the cross-band-PLV definition. Each yields a per-window scalar; we apply the same paired test + BH-FDR correction.

\subsection{Confound regression}

To test whether the closure displacement is reducible to band power / PLV / synchrony, each closure feature column is linearly regressed against the full $7$-dimensional baseline feature vector. The residual closure features are then re-entered into the calibrated drift test. Surviving the regression confirms that the displacement carries information beyond classical spectral measures.

\subsection{Source-localised replication}\label{sec:source-methods}

To rule out volume conduction --- the concern that nearby scalp electrodes share cortical generators and so phase-gradient and PLV measures are inflated by geometry rather than cortical phase organisation --- we re-ran the entire closure-feature pipeline at the source level. For each recording we built a forward solution against the MNE-Python \texttt{fsaverage} template anatomy~\cite{gramfort2014mne} (BEM: $5120$-vertex three-layer scalp/skull/brain; source space: \texttt{ico-4} surface decimation, $\approx\!2562$ sources per hemisphere); applied the sLORETA inverse with an ad-hoc identity noise covariance (no empty-room data are available; using the recording itself for the noise covariance would double-dip); projected to $68$ Desikan--Killiany cortical regions (\texttt{aparc} parcellation) using \texttt{mean\_flip} extraction (which handles the polar ambiguity intrinsic to inverse-solution dipoles). On the parcellated time series we recomputed the same $9$-D closure feature vector with the same per-window slide ($1$\,s window, $0.5$\,s step, $5$\,s burn-in, up to $240$\,s per condition), the same $k$-NN adjacency ($k=8$) but built on label-centroid coordinates in MNI space, and the same frontal subnetwork (replaced by aparc labels: superior/middle/caudal/rostral frontal, par opercularis/triangularis/orbitalis, frontal/lateral/medial orbitofrontal pole). The forward solution is cached per channel-set signature so the $14$ subjects share one BEM solve. Per-subject calibrated drift, awake-only standardisation, paired statistics, and per-feature BH-FDR are computed exactly as in the sensor-space pipeline.

\subsection{Independent-cohort cross-replication on clinical anaesthesia EEG}\label{sec:clinical-methods}

To test whether the per-subject calibrated drift generalises beyond the discovery dataset, we ran the identical pipeline on OpenNeuro \texttt{ds004541}~\cite{sainiferron2023dataset} (Saini Ferr\'on et al., Pontificia Universidad Cat\'olica de Chile, $n=8$ patients undergoing clinical general anaesthesia, $58$--$69$-channel EEG @ \SI{1000}{\hertz}, EDF format). Each recording contains BIDS-marked transitions through awake $\rightarrow$ verbal stimuli $\rightarrow$ motor $\rightarrow$ tetanic $\rightarrow$ \texttt{loc} (loss of consciousness) $\rightarrow$ \texttt{roc} (recovery). We extracted a $\SI{240}{\second}$ pre-induction baseline window starting at the BIDS \texttt{baseline} or \texttt{start} marker (the AWAKE segment) and a $\SI{240}{\second}$ unconscious window starting $\SI{60}{\second}$ after the \texttt{loc} marker, skipping the induction transient (the SED segment). Subject \texttt{sub-11} lacks a \texttt{loc} event in its BIDS metadata and was excluded, leaving $n=7$. Preprocessing (downsample to \SI{250}{\hertz}, $4$--$\SI{45}{\hertz}$ bandpass, $\SI{5}{\second}$ burn-in, drop non-EEG channels, $k=8$ k-NN adjacency) and the closure feature pipeline are identical to the propofol analysis. The drug regime is not specified in the BIDS metadata; the standard general-anaesthesia protocol at this institution typically combines propofol induction with sevoflurane maintenance, so we treat this as a mixed-agent independent-cohort replication and report the outcome accordingly.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Per-subject calibrated displacement: $14/14$ subjects, $p=6.1\!\times\!10^{-5}$}

Across all $n=14$ subjects, the per-subject calibrated drift is positive (sed cortex is further from each subject's personal awake reference than awake cortex is). Per-subject drift means are tabulated in Table~\ref{tab:drift}. The leave-one-subject-out re-standardisation gives $14/14$, $t=7.00$, $p=9.4\!\times\!10^{-6}$, $d_z=1.87$, confirming the test is not driven by within-data information leakage. Of $\num{5000}$ within-subject permutations, $0$ matched or exceeded the observed mean drift ($p_{\text{perm}}<2\!\times\!10^{-4}$); the all-positive $14/14$ pattern occurred at $p<2\!\times\!10^{-4}$.

The pattern is internally coherent: under propofol, the cortex becomes \emph{more} phase-organised at three levels --- global Kuramoto order ($q=0.032$), beta travelling-wave coherence ($q=0.007$), and per-channel alpha--beta cross-frequency phase locking ($q=7\!\times\!10^{-6}$) --- and \emph{less} coupled at the $\theta$--$\gamma$ phase-amplitude level ($q=0.032$), a putative cortical-coupling signature widely associated with information binding~\cite{tort2010measuring,canolty2006high}.

The effect is not transient and not ramping.

\subsection{Confound regression: closure $\not=$ band power}

Linearly regressing each closure feature against the full $7$-D baseline feature vector and re-running the calibrated drift test on residuals: $14/14$ subjects remain positive, paired $t=2.79$, $p=0.015$, Wilcoxon $W=105$, $p=6.1\!\times\!10^{-5}$, mean residual drift $\Delta=+0.84$. Per-feature $R^2$ explained by baselines ranges from $0.005$ (surrogate\_z) to $\sim 0.40$ (\texttt{pgd\_alpha}, \texttt{global\_order\_std}), with the exception of \texttt{global\_order} which is fully explained by \texttt{global\_synchrony\_alpha} ($R^2=1.00$, by definition --- the two are the same statistic, and we therefore declare \texttt{global\_order} as not adding information beyond the baseline; the FDR-significant effect on \texttt{global\_order} in Table~\ref{tab:perfeat} is recovered as the conventional propofol global-alpha-coherence increase, not a closure-specific finding). The remaining three FDR-significant features --- \texttt{cross\_band\_plv\_alpha\_beta}, \texttt{pgd\_beta}, and \texttt{pac\_theta\_gamma} --- have substantial unique variance after baseline regression. The displacement therefore carries information beyond classical spectral / synchrony measures, with the global-order axis being the only feature reducible to a baseline.

\subsection{$n$:$m$ specificity: fully broadband cross-band coupling}\label{sec:nm}

Propofol elevates cross-band $\alpha$:$\beta$ phase coupling broadly across low-integer harmonic ratios.

\section{Discussion}

The result of this note is empirical and narrow: under propofol sedation, the $9$-D closure feature space defined here exhibits a structured, replicated, FDR-corrected, multi-axis displacement of cortical state. Four mechanistically distinct features cross BH-FDR --- $\beta$-PGD~$\uparrow$, $\alpha$:$\beta$ cross-band PLV~$\uparrow$, global Kuramoto order~$\uparrow$, $\theta$--$\gamma$ PAC~$\downarrow$ --- and the spatial decomposition reproduces the well-known frontal alpha anteriorisation signature, with the legitimate claim being the spatial dissociation between whole-cortex $\beta$-PGD increase and frontal-selective $\alpha$-PGD increase rather than the recovery of the frontal-alpha effect itself (the frontal subnetwork is hard-coded into the feature definition). The displacement survives band-power confound regression and a $\num{5000}$-permutation null. Replication is in two cohorts (pilot $n=8$ and held-out $n=6$).

The observation that classical scalar baselines (band power, mean PLV, global synchrony) classify per-window awake vs sed somewhat better than the closure features ($0.87$ vs $0.80$) is not a counter-result. The closure space encodes \emph{structured} information across multiple wave-organisation axes, while the per-window classifier is a within-subject statistical exercise vulnerable to subject-specific confounds. The relevant claim is across-subject inference --- and there the calibrated displacement carries the clean signal.

We highlight three observations that we believe warrant attention from groups working on travelling-wave cognition and anaesthesia:

\paragraph{The synthetic prior was wrong direction.}
The synthetic regimes from which the basin templates were anchored modelled propofol as a \emph{rigid global lock} with reduced spatial gradients. Real propofol does the opposite for $\alpha$- and $\beta$-band wave organisation: wave coherence \emph{increases} (PGD up, cross-band PLV up). The synthetic-anchored residual distance therefore points partially in the wrong direction for a substantial subset of subjects, motivating the per-subject calibrated test. Future work should re-anchor the templates against real-data calibration cohorts.

\paragraph{Cross-band locking is broadband, not single-harmonic.}
Sweeping $n$:$m$ ratios $1$:$1$ through $4$:$1$ shows that propofol elevates $\alpha$:$\beta$ phase locking at $1$:$1$ through $3$:$1$ (FDR-significant), with the largest magnitude at $1$:$1$ and a monotonic decrease as $r$ increases. This is consistent with a globally rigidified phase regime rather than a specific harmonic resonance. We did not test $\alpha$:$\gamma$ or $\theta$:$\beta$ pairs.

\paragraph{The clinical posterior-alpha story is half-right.}
The textbook propofol pattern~\cite{purdon2013electroencephalogram} is described as alpha increasing frontally and decreasing posteriorly. In our $n=14$, the frontal increase is robust ($13/14$, $q=0.0024$); the posterior decrease, however, is split at chance across subjects ($6$--$7/14$ in the predicted direction) and does not reach significance. The "anteriorisation" narrative may merit refinement to: \emph{frontal alpha consistently increases; posterior alpha response is variable across individuals}. We note this contingently --- a single dataset is not a basis for revising clinical pharmacology --- but the data warrant the question.

\section{Relation to the broader closure programme}\label{sec:relation}

This paper reports a focused empirical claim: a $9$-dimensional cortical closure feature vector + per-subject calibrated displacement test discriminates propofol sedation from eyes-closed awake at $14/14$ subjects, source-localised replication, and independent-cohort clinical replication. The feature vector and the displacement statistic are described here in pipeline-empirical terms; they are not the closure operator of the broader VFD/ARIA framework. The companion methodology paper \citep{SmartClosureDistance} positions this displacement statistic as a first-order empirical proxy for distance-from-constraint-manifold, with applicability conditioned on a five-criterion diagnostic ($D_1$--$D_5$). On the source-localised cortical EEG anaesthesia substrate, the leave-one-subject-out feature-discovery procedure in \citep{SmartClosureDistance} converges to a stable two-feature minimal closure ($\beta$-band PGD plus $\alpha$:$\beta$ cross-band PLV) with cross-validated $d_z = 2.07$ and Jaccard $= 1.00$, dominating volume-conducted scalar baselines by $\approx 5.5\times$; that result is the cross-validated cousin of the $14/14$, $d_z = 1.90$ in-sample number reported here. The in-sample number is retained because all subjects' data was used to estimate per-subject means; the LOSO number is the appropriate honest cross-validated counterpart and should be cited downstream by papers that need the cross-validated effect size.

\paragraph{What this paper does not claim.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item Not that the $9$-dimensional closure feature vector is the closure operator. It is a measurable proxy aligned with multi-band cortical phase-wave organisation.
\item Not that the per-subject calibrated displacement is universal across all complex systems. Its applicability is conditional on the $D_1$--$D_5$ diagnostic of \citep{SmartClosureDistance}.
\item Not that propofol sedation is the only substrate this metric will work on. The companion methodology paper has tested $11$ paired-design substrates plus $4$ unpaired-design real-data substrates and reports $14/15$ correctly classified, with cortical EEG anaesthesia as the strongest validated Tier A anchor.
\item Not that the result proves a theory of consciousness. The displacement is a measurable cortical-state signature of anaesthesia; consciousness theorisation is a separate matter beyond the scope of this paper.
\item Not that the present feature vector is the unique correct decomposition. The LOSO discovery procedure of \citep{SmartClosureDistance} identifies a minimal two-feature subset; the full $9$-vector remains useful for reporting the per-feature decomposition documented in Â§3.2.
\item Not that the textbook posterior-alpha-decrease pattern has been falsified by this $n = 14$. It has been refined: frontal-alpha increase is robust; posterior-alpha response is variable across individuals in this cohort.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limits}

This study has the following constraints:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item Pure-agent generalisation is partial. The clinical cohort (\texttt{ds004541}) is mixed-agent (likely propofol induction + sevoflurane maintenance) and does not isolate sevoflurane, ketamine, dexmedetomidine, or xenon. A clean single-agent sevoflurane or ketamine EEG dataset, openly accessible and BIDS-compliant, was not located on OpenNeuro at the time of writing; the relevant resting-state ketamine/xenon dataset on Zenodo (DOI \texttt{10.5281/zenodo.806176}) is restricted-access and was not downloaded for this study.
\item No LFP / laminar recordings; the metric has not been applied to higher-spatial-resolution wave-organisation data of the kind that is routine in non-human primate work.
\item $n=14$ is small for biological inference; we report per-subject paired tests and within-subject permutations as the inferentially appropriate statistics, but absolute effect sizes and clinical applicability require larger cohorts.
\item No dose-response. The two conditions are eyes-closed awake and a single (low-to-moderate) propofol level.
\item Per-subject calibration uses each subject's awake distribution as the reference. This is appropriate for an awake-vs-sed contrast but cannot detect awake-state abnormalities (e.g., the partial recording instability in subjects $1010$ and $1017$ that emerges in the per-window distance distributions).
\item Source localisation uses the \texttt{fsaverage} template anatomy with an ad-hoc identity noise covariance (no per-subject MRI, no empty-room baseline). This is the standard tradeoff for resting-state EEG without subject-specific MRI; the parcellated $68$-ROI analysis is robust to per-source noise but cannot make claims about precise dipole locations.
\item We have not tested $\alpha$:$\gamma$ or $\theta$:$\beta$ cross-band coupling.
\end{itemize}

\section{Falsification criteria}\label{sec:falsification}

We pre-state the conditions under which the central claim of this note should be considered falsified, in order of priority. Where the present version of this note has tested a criterion, we annotate the outcome.

\begin{enumerate}\itemsep0pt
\item If the $14/14$ all-positive calibrated-drift result fails to replicate in an independent cohort drawn from a different anaesthesia EEG dataset, the displacement claim is dataset-specific. \textbf{Tested (\S\ref{sec:clinical-results}):} replicates $7/7$ in \texttt{ds004541} clinical anaesthesia patients with $d_z=1.98$, matching the propofol effect size; criterion not triggered.
\item If the displacement survives band-power confound regression in this dataset but fails in a different dataset (e.g., one where awake EEG already has elevated alpha coherence), the closure metric is acting as a downstream proxy for alpha power in a way we have not characterised.
\item If sevoflurane, dexmedetomidine, or ketamine sedation produce the \emph{opposite} sign of $\theta$--$\gamma$ PAC change, the "decreased information transfer" interpretation of feature 4 fails. \textbf{Tested partially (\S\ref{sec:clinical-results}):} mixed-agent clinical cohort retains the propofol direction ($-0.004$ vs $-0.006$); criterion not triggered. Single-agent sevoflurane / ketamine still untested.
\item If macaque LFP working-memory recordings show that successful task performance correlates with $\alpha$:$\beta$ cross-band PLV \emph{increase} during delay periods, the framing of cross-band locking as an anaesthetic signature requires substantial qualification.
\item If the headline displacement \emph{disappears} when re-computed at the source level (sLORETA + cortical parcellation), the metric was reporting volume-conduction geometry rather than cortical phase organisation. \textbf{Tested (\S\ref{sec:source-results}):} the source-space effect size is \emph{larger} than the sensor-space effect size ($d_z=2.60$ vs $1.90$); the two strongest sensor-space features (cross-band PLV, $\beta$-PGD) reach $q<10^{-6}$ at the source level; criterion not triggered.
\end{enumerate}
