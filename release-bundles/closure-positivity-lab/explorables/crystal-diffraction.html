Three point-sets, three diffraction patterns — and Dyson's question.

Shine waves through a structure and read the spectrum: periodic order gives sharp peaks; randomness gives noise. In 1982 quasicrystals showed a third possibility — sharp peaks with no period. Compare the golden-ratio chain with the primes.

1 — Fibonacci φ-chain (computed live: 3,000 tiles, lengths φ and 1)

Sharp peaks, never repeating: the canonical 1-D quasicrystal. Golden-ratio geometry — the native territory of this repository's icosian programme.

2 — The same number of points at random (computed live)

No order, no peaks: featureless noise at intensity ≈ 1.

3 — The primes (precomputed: p < 10⁶ on the log-line, smooth taper)

Orange: the primes' spectrum. Dashed green: the zeta zeros, computed independently in PARI/GP from the zeta function — every in-range zero aligns with a local peak. Dyson's proposal (2009): under the Riemann Hypothesis the zeros form a one-dimensional quasicrystal in a precise generalized sense (the singular part of their Fourier transform sits exactly on the prime-power logarithms) — so classify the one-dimensional quasicrystals, and find the zeros in the catalogue. The classification is open. No claim about it is made here.

Panels 1–2 are computed in your browser when the page loads (fixed seed, so everyone sees the same figure). Panel 3 is generated by explorables/make_data.py from the same sieve as the prime phenomena ledger, and the zero positions come from out/zeta_zeros.txt — two independent computations meeting on one plot.
