Primes ≡ 3 (mod 4) stay ahead of primes ≡ 1 (mod 4) — 99.93% of the way to fifty million.

Chebyshev noticed it in 1853. The lead D(x) = π(x; 4,3) − π(x; 4,1) is plotted below from a real sieve. In explicit-formula language the cause is interference: the wave of prime squares loads the residue-1 side only, displacing the race by about √x / log x — an asymmetric fringe.

Around x = 26,861 the lead dips below zero for the first time — for a handful of primes — then recovers for hundreds of thousands more.

Real data: every point is the exact value of D at a prime, computed by explorables/make_data.py from the same sieve as ledger row 5 (closure-positivity-lab, out/prime_ledger.json). What is proven: the bias exists and its first reversal is at 26,861. What is open: even that the lead-set has a density is unproven without GRH + linear independence (Rubinstein–Sarnak 1994 give 0.9959 under those hypotheses). No claim beyond the computation is made here.
