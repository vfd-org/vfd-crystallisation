\begin{abstract}
The three-order baryon-lepton mass law of the companion paper~\citep{SmartI} achieves structural agreement for the proton-to-electron ratio at the $2 \times 10^{-4}$ level but cannot accommodate heavier lepton masses under shell-support extension. We prove a formal no-go: the combinatorial invariant $C = k! - k(k{+}1)/2 - 1$ for boundary-starting shell supports grows factorially, and no support of any size produces mass ratios in the muon or tau range. This establishes that lepton generations require a mass contribution independent of shell support. We identify this contribution as a winding-dependent boundary excitation operator $f(w) = \phig^N (w{-}1)^{1/\phig}$, where the exponent $1/\phig$ is consistent with hydrogen-like spectral scaling in a space of effective dimension~$\phig$, linked to the manifold's defining ratio by the exact identity $\phig/(\phig{+}1) = 1/\phig$, and the coefficient $\phig^N$, with $N$ the manifold shell count, is the unique phi-integer-power matching the muon mass to $0.1\%$. With zero fitted continuous parameters, the operator predicts the muon-to-electron ratio to $0.5\%$ and the tau-to-electron ratio to $3.7\%$. The spectral-dimension assumption and the coefficient's structural origin remain open for future derivation. Together with~\citep{SmartI}, the framework provides a structural candidate architecture covering the proton/electron hierarchy and a constrained extension to three lepton generations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

A companion paper~\citep{SmartI} establishes a three-order mass law for the baryon-lepton hierarchy within the deterministic closure framework, achieving structural agreement for the proton-to-electron mass ratio at the $\sim\!2 \times 10^{-4}$ level with zero fitted continuous parameters. That law, however, cannot reproduce heavier lepton masses: the present paper shows that this is not a technical limitation but a structural necessity, provable as a no-go theorem. \section{No-Go Theorem for Shell-Extension Leptons}\label{sec:nogo}
%==================================================================

\begin{proposition}[Shell-Extension No-Go]\label{prop:nogo}
Under the three-order mass law of~\citep{SmartI} with assignment rules R1--R5, no boundary-starting shell support $S = \{1, 2, \ldots, k\}$ produces a mass ratio $m(S)/m_e$ in the interval $[100, 4000]$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For $S = \{1, \ldots, k\}$, the combinatorial invariant is $C(S) = k! - k(k{+}1)/2 - 1$. The three-order exponent relative to the electron is:
\[
\Delta\mathcal{E}(k) = [C(\{1,\ldots,k\}) - C(\{1\})] + \Delta g_1 + \Delta g_2
\]

Direct computation gives:

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Mass ratios under shell-support extension from boundary.}
\label{tab:nogo}
\begin{tabular}{@{}ccccc@{}}
\toprule
$k$ & $C$ & $\Delta\mathcal{E}$ & Predicted ratio & Observed target \\
\midrule
1 & $-1$ & 0 & 1.00 & (electron) \\
2 & $-2$ & $-0.50$ & 0.79 & muon: 206.8 \\
3 & $-1$ & $+0.62$ & 1.35 & tau: 3477 \\
4 & $13$ & $+14.70$ & 1182 & --- \\
5 & $104$ & $+105.76$ & $>10^{21}$ & --- \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The function $k! - k(k{+}1)/2 - 1$ grows super-exponentially: $C$ jumps from $-1$ (at $k = 3$) to $13$ (at $k = 4$), producing a ratio gap from $\sim\!1$ to $\sim\!1200$ with no intermediate values. The muon ($\sim\!207$) and tau ($\sim\!3477$) mass ratios lie in this gap. No integer $k$ reaches the interval $[100, 4000]$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
Lepton generations require a mass-lifting mechanism independent of shell-support size.
\end{corollary}

\section{Identification of the Missing Mechanism}\label{sec:identification}
%==================================================================

The no-go constrains the missing mechanism. It must:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item generate mass ratios of $\sim\!10^2$--$10^3$ for boundary leptons,
    \item not modify the proton-to-electron ratio (which uses winding $w = 1$ for both particles),
    \item be discrete (to preserve the discrete particle spectrum),
    \item have a structural origin within the closure framework.
\end{enumerate}

The only remaining structural degree of freedom for a boundary lepton (shell $\{1\}$, fixed by R1--R2) is the \textit{winding number} $w$. Under the original rule R4, leptons are restricted to $w = 1$. Extending R4 to allow $w > 1$ for higher lepton generations gives:
\begin{align}
    \text{electron:} \quad & \{1\},\; w = 1 \quad \text{(ground state)} \\
    \text{muon:} \quad & \{1\},\; w = 2 \quad \text{(first excitation)} \\
    \text{tau:} \quad & \{1\},\; w = 3 \quad \text{(second excitation)}
\end{align}

This reinterpretation preserves the proton-to-electron law exactly: the proton and electron both have $w = 1$, so any winding-dependent contribution $f(w)$ satisfying $f(1) = 0$ leaves the Paper~I result unchanged.

\section{The Winding Excitation Operator}\label{sec:operator}

\subsection{Operator Form}

The winding contribution to the mass exponent is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fw}
    f(w) = \phig^N \cdot (w - 1)^{1/\phig}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the manifold shell count and $\phig = (1+\sqrt{5})/2$. This satisfies $f(1) = 0$ by construction.

\subsection{Status of the Exponent $1/\phig$}

The exponent $1/\phig$ is consistent with the standard spectral scaling for hydrogen-like excitations in a space of effective dimension $d$, where the $w$-th level scales as $w^{d/(d+1)}$. If the effective spectral dimension of the $\phig$-manifold equals $\phig$, then:
\[
\frac{d}{d+1} = \frac{\phig}{\phig+1} = \frac{1}{\phig}
\]
The last equality is the \textit{defining property of the golden ratio}: $\phig = 1 + 1/\phig$, so $\phig/(\phig+1) = 1/\phig$. This identity is exact.

\begin{remark}[Derivation Status]
The identity $\phig/(\phig+1) = 1/\phig$ is mathematically exact. The assumption that the $\phig$-manifold has effective spectral dimension $\phig$ is structurally motivated --- it is the manifold's defining scaling ratio --- but has not been independently derived from the closure geometry. The exponent $1/\phig$ is therefore \textit{conditionally derived}: it follows rigorously from the spectral-dimension assumption, and that assumption is linked to the manifold's defining property, but establishing $d_s = \phig$ from the manifold's Laplacian remains open.
\end{remark}

\section{Unified Law}\label{sec:unified}
%==================================================================

The extended mass law, incorporating the winding operator alongside the Paper~I baryon-lepton law, takes the factorised form:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:unified}
    m(\Acal) = \Lambda \;\cdot\; \phig^{C(\Acal)} \;\cdot\; \phig^{|E|/|V|} \;\cdot\; \phig^{-\mathrm{Var}(\deg)\,|E|/|V|^2} \;\cdot\; \phig^{f(w)}
\end{equation}
where $|E|$ and $|V|$ are the edge and vertex counts of the closure graph, and $f(w)$ is defined by Eq.~\eqref{eq:fw}. Each factor acts on an independent structural sector: shell indices, graph connectivity, degree heterogeneity, and winding excitation. Independent sectors multiply at the mass level, yielding additive exponents at leading order.

For particles with $w = 1$ (electron, proton, neutron), the winding factor equals $\phig^0 = 1$ and the law reduces to Paper~I. For $w > 1$ (muon, tau), the winding factor provides the generation-lifting contribution that the shell-support law cannot supply.

Neutron-proton splitting, which requires a symmetry-sector correction $\phig^{\delta_\sigma}$ with $\delta_\sigma \approx 0.003$, would enter as a fifth independent factor. This is compatible with the architecture but not yet derived.

\section{Scope and Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}
%==================================================================

\textbf{What is derived within the framework:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The no-go theorem for shell-extension leptons (fully derived).
    \item The necessity of a winding-dependent operator (consequence of no-go).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What is conditionally derived:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The exponent $1/\phig$ (follows from spectral scaling under the assumption $d_s = \phig$).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What is structurally motivated:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The coefficient $\phig^5$ (unique in $\phig^k$ family; interpreted as manifold depth).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What remains open:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Independent derivation of the effective spectral dimension $d_s = \phig$.
    \item Derivation of the coefficient from the closure geometry rather than from the $\phig^k$ uniqueness criterion.
    \item Neutron-proton mass splitting.
    \item Extension to quarks and heavier hadrons.
    \item Physical realisation of the $\phig$-manifold.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What this paper does not claim:}
A first-principles derivation of the winding operator. The operator is constrained by the no-go and by structural consistency, not derived from an underlying action principle. The $3.7\%$ tau error is the framework's current structural precision, not a metrological prediction.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%==================================================================

The central result of this paper is the no-go theorem: shell-support extension cannot produce lepton generations. This is not a weakness of the framework but a structural feature that identifies the correct missing mechanism.

The winding excitation operator $f(w) = \phig^N (w{-}1)^{1/\phig}$ is the strongest constrained candidate:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the exponent follows from an exact identity of the golden ratio under a stated spectral assumption,
    \item the coefficient is uniquely privileged within its natural family,
    \item the operator preserves all Paper~I results exactly,
    \item the tau prediction is a genuine test (not a calibration).
\end{itemize}

The agreement obtained here is structural rather than metrological. The muon mass is known to much higher precision than $0.5\%$; the tau to much higher than $3.7\%$. The framework provides leading-order structural agreement, not high-precision predictions.

Whether the spectral-dimension assumption $d_s = \phig$ can be independently established from the manifold's closure geometry is the primary remaining formal question. If it can, the exponent $1/\phig$ becomes fully derived, upgrading the claim level of the entire programme.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%==================================================================

A formal no-go theorem establishes that lepton generations cannot be produced by shell-support extension within the closure-class mass law: the combinatorial invariant's factorial growth skips the muon and tau mass window entirely. The required winding-dependent boundary excitation operator $f(w) = \phig^N (w{-}1)^{1/\phig}$ is identified by structural constraint rather than parameter fitting. The exponent $1/\phig$ follows from hydrogen-like spectral scaling under the assumption that the $\phig$-manifold's effective spectral dimension equals $\phig$; this assumption is linked to the manifold's defining property ($\phig/(\phig{+}1) = 1/\phig$) but has not been independently established. The coefficient $\phig^N$ is the unique phi-integer-power matching the muon mass and is interpreted as the total manifold depth, though this interpretation awaits derivation from the closure geometry.

The operator predicts the muon-to-electron ratio to $0.5\%$ and the tau-to-electron ratio to $3.7\%$ with zero fitted continuous parameters. Together with the proton-to-electron law of~\citep{SmartI}, the framework provides a constrained structural extension from the proton/electron hierarchy to the three charged leptons, with two formal ingredients still open: the spectral-dimension assumption and the coefficient derivation.
