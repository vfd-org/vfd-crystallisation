\begin{abstract}
Paper~XIV introduced stochastic closure dynamics, producing a probabilistic layer over the closure landscape. However, that layer is dissipative and real-valued: it lacks phase, interference, and the oscillatory structure characteristic of quantum mechanics. The present paper extends the closure framework by augmenting the state space with a phase variable, introducing candidate dynamical formulations that incorporate phase evolution alongside closure descent, and demonstrating that interference arises naturally when multiple closure-compatible paths contribute to the transition amplitude between configurations.

Three candidate formulations are developed and compared: (A) a complexified gradient flow combining closure descent with phase rotation, (B) a Hamiltonian lift embedding closure dynamics in a conservative phase space, and (C) a path-integral formulation with explicit phase weighting. A worked double-well example demonstrates that path-dependent phase accumulation produces constructive and destructive interference in the transition amplitude. Crystallisation is extended to phase-coherent selection: the realised state is the configuration that maximises both closure compatibility and phase coherence.

The paper does not claim equivalence with quantum mechanics. It establishes the first phase-bearing dynamical layer within the closure framework, demonstrates that interference is a structural consequence of phase-augmented closure dynamics, and identifies the conditions under which the system reduces to the dissipative gradient flow of Paper~XII in the classical limit.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%==================================================================

Papers~XII--XIV~\citep{SmartXII, SmartXIII, SmartXIV} established a dynamical and probabilistic layer for the closure framework: deterministic gradient flow, multi-state interaction via landscape deformation, and stochastic occupation with a Boltzmann-like stationary distribution. These results provide attractor structure, transition rates, and path-integral formulation --- but they are fundamentally \textit{dissipative and real-valued}. They lack:

\begin{itemize}
    \item phase variables and oscillatory dynamics,
    \item interference between alternative paths,
    \item non-classical probability patterns arising from phase coherence.
\end{itemize}

These are the features that distinguish quantum from classical probabilistic behaviour. The present paper introduces a phase-augmented state space and explores dynamical formulations that produce interference from the closure structure.

\subsection*{Scope and epistemic status}

This paper introduces \textit{candidate formulations} for phase-coherent closure dynamics. It does not claim equivalence with quantum mechanics, does not postulate Hilbert-space axioms, and does not derive the Schr\"odinger equation. The phase structure is introduced as a minimal extension of the closure dynamics; whether it can reproduce the full structure of quantum mechanics is an open question.

%==================================================================
\section{Limitations of Stochastic Closure Dynamics}\label{sec:limitations_XIV}
%==================================================================

The stochastic dynamics of Paper~XIV produces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a stationary distribution $P_{\mathrm{st}}(\psi) \propto \exp(-2\Fcal(\psi)/\sigma^2)$,
    \item Kramers-type transition rates between attractor basins,
    \item an Onsager--Machlup path functional with real-valued weights.
\end{itemize}

However, this structure is thermodynamic rather than quantum:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the path weights $e^{-S/\sigma^2}$ are real and positive --- there is no cancellation between paths,
    \item the stationary distribution is a Boltzmann measure, not a squared amplitude,
    \item no interference arises: the probability of reaching a configuration is the sum of path probabilities, not the squared modulus of a sum of amplitudes.
\end{itemize}

To produce quantum-like behaviour, the framework must be extended to include phase.

\section{Phase-Augmented State Space}\label{sec:phase}
%==================================================================

\begin{definition}[Phase-augmented state]\label{def:augmented}
The phase-augmented state space is:
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{\mathcal{S}}_{\mathrm{adm}} = \Sadm \times S^1
\end{equation}
with elements $\Psi = (\psi, \theta)$, where $\psi \in \Sadm$ is the closure configuration and $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ is the phase variable.
\end{definition}

Equivalently, one may write the augmented state in polar form:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:polar}
    \Psi = A(\psi)\, e^{i\theta}
\end{equation}
where $A(\psi) \geq 0$ is an amplitude derived from the closure structure (a natural candidate is $A(\psi) = e^{-\Fcal(\psi)/\sigma^2}$, consistent with the stationary distribution of Paper~XIV).

\begin{remark}
The phase variable $\theta$ is not postulated as a quantum-mechanical phase. It is introduced as a structural degree of freedom on the closure landscape. Whether it can be identified with the quantum phase is an open question.
\end{remark}

\section{Candidate Dynamical Formulations}\label{sec:formulations}
%==================================================================

We develop and compare three candidate formulations for phase-coherent closure dynamics. Each extends the base gradient flow of Paper~XII by incorporating phase evolution.

\subsection{Formulation A: Complexified gradient flow}

The simplest phase extension combines closure descent with phase rotation:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:formA}
    \dot{\Psi} = -\nabla\Fcal(\psi)\, \Psi + i\, \Omega(\psi)\, \Psi
\end{equation}
where $\Omega(\psi) \geq 0$ is a \textit{phase-rotation frequency} determined by the closure structure. The real part drives the system toward closure; the imaginary part rotates the phase.

\textbf{Candidate for $\Omega$:} A natural choice is $\Omega(\psi) = \Fcal(\psi)$ or $\Omega(\psi) = \mathrm{tr}(H(\psi))$ (trace of the Hessian), so that the phase rotation rate is determined by the local closure structure. On $\Mcl$ (where $\Fcal = 0$), the phase rotation vanishes or is governed by the Hessian alone.

\textbf{Properties:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item reduces to Paper~XII gradient flow when $\Omega = 0$ (or when phase is ignored),
    \item the modulus $|\Psi|$ evolves as $d|\Psi|/dt = -\nabla\Fcal \cdot |\Psi|$ (dissipative, as before),
    \item the phase evolves as $\dot{\theta} = \Omega(\psi)$ (non-dissipative rotation).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Formulation B: Hamiltonian lift}

A conservative formulation embeds closure dynamics in a canonical phase space:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:HamiltonianB}
    \Hcal(\psi, \theta) = \Fcal(\psi) + \Phi(\psi, \theta)
\end{equation}
with canonical equations:
\begin{align}
    \dot{\psi} &= \frac{\partial \Hcal}{\partial \theta} = \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \theta} \label{eq:canpsi} \\
    \dot{\theta} &= -\frac{\partial \Hcal}{\partial \psi} = -\nabla\Fcal(\psi) - \frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial \psi} \label{eq:cantheta}
\end{align}

\textbf{Candidate for $\Phi$:} If $\Phi = p \cdot \theta$ (where $p$ is a conjugate momentum), the system becomes a standard Hamiltonian system with $\Fcal$ as the potential energy. If $\Phi = 0$, the equations degenerate and the system is not fully canonical.

\textbf{Properties:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item energy $\Hcal$ is conserved (no dissipation),
    \item phase evolves according to the closure gradient,
    \item provides a natural bridge to the Lagrangian formulation of Paper~XII.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Limitation:} The Hamiltonian lift requires a careful definition of the conjugate structure between $\psi$ and $\theta$, which is not uniquely determined in the present paper.

\subsection{Formulation C: Path-integral phase weighting}

The most direct route to interference modifies the Onsager--Machlup path functional of Paper~XIV by adding an explicit phase:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:formC}
    \mathcal{A}[\psi(\cdot)] = \exp\!\left(-\frac{S[\psi]}{\sigma^2}\right) \cdot \exp\!\left(i\, \Theta[\psi(\cdot)]\right)
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $S[\psi] = \int_0^T \|\dot{\psi} + \nabla\Fcal(\psi)\|^2\, dt$ is the Onsager--Machlup action (real, positive),
    \item $\Theta[\psi(\cdot)] = \int_0^T \Omega(\psi(t))\, dt$ is the accumulated phase along the path.
\end{itemize}

The total amplitude for reaching configuration $\psi_f$ from $\psi_i$ is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:totalamp}
    \mathcal{A}(\psi_i \to \psi_f) = \sum_{\text{paths}} \mathcal{A}[\psi(\cdot)]
\end{equation}

The probability is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:probability}
    P(\psi_i \to \psi_f) = \left|\mathcal{A}(\psi_i \to \psi_f)\right|^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Properties:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item recovers Paper~XIV when $\Theta = 0$ (no phase): $P = \sum |e^{-S/\sigma^2}|^2$ (incoherent sum),
    \item produces interference when $\Theta \neq 0$: different paths contribute with different phases,
    \item the structure is formally analogous to the Feynman path integral with $e^{iS/\hbar}$, except that the real damping factor $e^{-S/\sigma^2}$ is retained.
\end{itemize}

\begin{remark}[Assessment]
Formulation~C is the most direct route to interference: it explicitly produces path-dependent phase contributions and reduces to Paper~XIV in the phase-free limit. Formulations~A and~B provide complementary perspectives (differential-equation and Hamiltonian, respectively) and may be more suitable for specific applications. The present paper develops Formulation~C as the primary framework and uses A and B as supporting structures.
\end{remark}

\section{Path Interference and Amplitude Structure}\label{sec:interference}
%==================================================================

\subsection{Interference condition}

Consider two distinct paths $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ from $\psi_i$ to $\psi_f$, with actions $S_1, S_2$ and accumulated phases $\Theta_1, \Theta_2$. The total amplitude is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{tot}} = e^{-S_1/\sigma^2 + i\Theta_1} + e^{-S_2/\sigma^2 + i\Theta_2}
\end{equation}

The probability is:
\begin{align}\label{eq:interference}
    P &= |\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{tot}}|^2 \notag \\
    &= e^{-2S_1/\sigma^2} + e^{-2S_2/\sigma^2} + 2\, e^{-(S_1+S_2)/\sigma^2}\, \cos(\Theta_1 - \Theta_2)
\end{align}

The third term is the \textit{interference term}. It vanishes when $\Theta_1 = \Theta_2$ (no phase difference) and oscillates when $\Theta_1 \neq \Theta_2$.

\begin{proposition}[Interference from phase-augmented closure dynamics]\label{prop:interference}
If two paths $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ from $\psi_i$ to $\psi_f$ have different accumulated phases $\Theta_1 \neq \Theta_2$, then:
\begin{equation}
    |\mathcal{A}_1 + \mathcal{A}_2|^2 \neq |\mathcal{A}_1|^2 + |\mathcal{A}_2|^2
\end{equation}
That is, the transition probability exhibits interference.
\end{proposition}

This is the central result of the paper: \textit{interference arises as a structural consequence of phase-augmented closure dynamics whenever the phase-rotation frequency $\Omega$ is path-dependent}.

\section{Worked Example: Double-Well System}\label{sec:example}
%==================================================================

\begin{example}[Double-well interference]\label{ex:doublewell}
Consider a one-dimensional closure functional with two symmetric minima:
\begin{equation}
    \Fcal(\psi) = \lambda\, (\psi^2 - a^2)^2
\end{equation}
with minima at $\psi = \pm a$ and a barrier at $\psi = 0$ of height $\Delta\Fcal = \lambda a^4$.

Two paths connect $\psi_i = -a$ to $\psi_f = +a$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\gamma_+$: passes over the barrier through $\psi = 0$ (direct crossing),
    \item $\gamma_-$: wraps around through a longer route in state space (if the topology permits).
\end{itemize}

Each path accumulates a different phase:
\begin{equation}
    \Theta_\pm = \int_{\gamma_\pm} \Omega(\psi(t))\, dt
\end{equation}

The transition amplitude is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{A} = e^{-S_+/\sigma^2 + i\Theta_+} + e^{-S_-/\sigma^2 + i\Theta_-}
\end{equation}

The probability exhibits interference:
\begin{equation}
    P = e^{-2S_+/\sigma^2} + e^{-2S_-/\sigma^2} + 2\, e^{-(S_+ + S_-)/\sigma^2}\, \cos(\Theta_+ - \Theta_-)
\end{equation}

When $\Theta_+ - \Theta_- = 0$: constructive interference (maximum probability).\\
When $\Theta_+ - \Theta_- = \pi$: destructive interference (minimum probability).

This demonstrates that the phase-augmented closure dynamics produces non-classical probability structure from the closure landscape alone.
\end{example}

\section{Crystallisation Under Phase Coherence}\label{sec:crystal}
%==================================================================

In Paper~XIV, crystallisation was defined as the convergence of stochastic trajectories toward closure-stable attractor basins. With phase structure, crystallisation must also account for phase coherence.

\begin{definition}[Phase-coherent crystallisation]\label{def:phasecrystal}
A configuration $\Psi^* = (\psi^*, \theta^*)$ is phase-coherently crystallised if:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\psi^*$ is closure-stable: $\Fcal(\psi^*) = 0$,
    \item the phase $\theta^*$ is stable under the phase dynamics: $\dot{\theta} = \Omega(\psi^*)$ is constant or zero,
    \item the configuration is a local maximum of the phase-weighted probability $|\mathcal{A}|^2$ over the accessible basin.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The realised state is the configuration that maximises both closure compatibility and phase coherence. States that are closure-stable but phase-incoherent (rapidly varying $\theta$) are suppressed; states that are both closure-stable and phase-stable are selected.

%==================================================================
\section{Toward Probability Emergence}\label{sec:probability}
%==================================================================

The transition probability~\eqref{eq:probability}:
\begin{equation}
    P(\psi_i \to \psi_f) = \left|\sum_{\text{paths}} \mathcal{A}[\psi(\cdot)]\right|^2
\end{equation}
has the form of a squared modulus of a sum of complex amplitudes. This is structurally identical to the Born rule applied to a sum of quantum amplitudes.

\begin{remark}[Born-like structure]
The squared-modulus form arises naturally from the path-integral formulation~C: it is a consequence of the complex-valued amplitude structure, not a postulate. However, the identification of this structure with the quantum-mechanical Born rule requires:
\begin{itemize}
    \item that the phase-rotation frequency $\Omega$ be related to a physical energy or Hamiltonian,
    \item that the damping factor $e^{-S/\sigma^2}$ be negligible or factorable for the relevant paths,
    \item that the sum over paths be dominated by the classical path plus fluctuations.
\end{itemize}
These conditions are not established in the present paper. The squared-modulus structure is a structural feature of the formulation, not yet a derived physical law.
\end{remark}

\section{Classical Limit}\label{sec:classical}
%==================================================================

The phase-augmented dynamics reduces to the dissipative gradient flow of Paper~XII in the following limits:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Phase-free limit ($\Omega \to 0$):} the phase variable becomes static ($\dot{\theta} = 0$), all path phases are equal ($\Theta_1 = \Theta_2$), interference vanishes, and the dynamics reduces to $\dot{\psi} = -\nabla\Fcal(\psi)$.

    \item \textbf{Decoherence limit ($\sigma \to \infty$):} the damping factor $e^{-S/\sigma^2} \to 1$ for all paths, the amplitude becomes pure phase, and in the presence of environmental noise the phase differences average out, destroying interference.

    \item \textbf{Single-path dominance ($\sigma \to 0$):} only the path of minimum action contributes, the sum over paths collapses to a single term, and the dynamics becomes deterministic gradient flow.
\end{enumerate}

In each limit, the system reduces to the classical closure dynamics of Paper~XII. The phase-coherent structure is an extension, not a replacement.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:discussion}
%==================================================================

\subsection{What is achieved}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Phase has been introduced into the closure framework as a structural degree of freedom.
    \item Three candidate dynamical formulations have been defined and compared.
    \item Interference has been demonstrated as a structural consequence of path-dependent phase accumulation.
    \item Crystallisation has been extended to include phase coherence.
    \item The classical limit reduces to Paper~XII gradient flow.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What is not achieved}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{No equivalence with QM.} The framework shares structural features with quantum mechanics but is not equivalent: unitarity is absent, the damping factor is retained, and the Born rule is not derived.
    \item \textbf{$\Omega$ is not uniquely determined.} The phase-rotation frequency is a structural input, not derived from first principles.
    \item \textbf{The path-integral sum is formal.} The sum over paths is defined structurally but not computed for realistic systems.
    \item \textbf{No Schr\"odinger equation.} No differential equation equivalent to the Schr\"odinger equation is derived.
    \item \textbf{No Hilbert-space structure.} The augmented state space is $\Sadm \times S^1$, not a Hilbert space.
    \item \textbf{The double-well example is schematic.} It demonstrates the mechanism but does not compute numerical predictions for a physical system.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Open questions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Can $\Omega$ be derived from the closure structure (e.g.\ from the Hessian eigenvalues)?
    \item Can the damping factor $e^{-S/\sigma^2}$ be absorbed into a redefined measure, leaving a purely oscillatory path integral?
    \item Is there a Wick rotation connecting the damped closure dynamics to an oscillatory quantum dynamics?
    \item Can the augmented state space be promoted to a Hilbert space with a suitable inner product?
\end{itemize}

%==================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%==================================================================

The closure framework, previously dissipative and real-valued, now admits a phase-coherent extension. Phase is introduced as a structural degree of freedom; candidate dynamical formulations combine closure descent with phase rotation; and interference arises as a structural consequence of path-dependent phase accumulation in the path-integral formulation.

The double-well example demonstrates constructive and destructive interference from the closure landscape alone, without quantum-mechanical postulates. Crystallisation is extended to phase-coherent selection, and the classical limit reduces to the gradient flow of Paper~XII.

This paper does not claim quantum mechanics. It claims something narrower but still significant: that the closure framework, augmented with phase, produces interference --- the signature feature that distinguishes quantum from classical probability. Whether this phase structure can be developed into a full recovery of quantum mechanics, or whether it represents a genuinely distinct probabilistic framework, is the central open question for the programme.
