\begin{abstract}
Papers~XXIII--XXV derived event ordering, observer-dependent kinematics, and separation geometry from phase-overlap structure without assuming spacetime. The present paper addresses the next question: when is that geometry uniform, and when is it distorted?

Flatness is defined as local uniformity of event neighborhood profiles in the transition graph: a region is flat if nearby events have identical local accessibility profiles. Curvature is defined as deviation from this local uniformity. Three curvature candidates are constructed and compared: (A)~volume-growth distortion --- deviation of metric-ball sizes from a local reference; (B)~branching asymmetry --- non-uniformity of the directed neighborhood structure; (C)~geodesic concentration --- preferential routing of shortest paths through specific events.

Two toy models are worked explicitly: a symmetric two-layer model (flat, all curvature indicators vanish) and an asymmetric three-layer model with a bottleneck (curved, all three indicators are nonzero). In the curved model, geodesics concentrate through the bottleneck, providing a free-fall analogue: motion follows the accessibility structure of the event geometry without any imposed force.

The paper does not derive Einstein equations or curvature tensors. It establishes a concrete curvature precursor on the event-order framework, showing that non-uniform event accessibility produces detectable geometric distortion.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%==================================================================

Paper~XXV~\citep{SmartXXV} established three separation constructions on the event poset: chain-length separation for comparable events, observer-disagreement for incomparable events, and a transition-cost metric on the full Hasse graph. These give the event set a metric-like geometry.

The next question is: is that geometry uniform everywhere, or can it be distorted? In smooth general relativity, the answer is curvature: the Riemann tensor measures how the geometry deviates from flatness. The present paper constructs the discrete analogue: curvature as non-uniformity of local event accessibility.

\subsection*{Central principle}

\begin{quote}
In VFD, curvature is the failure of local event accessibility to remain uniform.
\end{quote}

\subsection*{Scope}

This paper defines flatness, constructs curvature indicators, and demonstrates them on toy models. It does not derive Einstein equations, curvature tensors, or continuous geometry. It establishes a curvature precursor on the discrete event-order framework.

\section{Local Neighborhoods and Flatness}\label{sec:flat}
%==================================================================

\subsection{Metric balls}

\begin{definition}[Metric ball]\label{def:ball}
For event $e \in \Ecal$ and radius $r \geq 0$, the metric ball in the transition graph $\Gcal$ is:
\begin{equation}
    B_r(e) = \{e' \in \Ecal : d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e, e') \leq r\}
\end{equation}
The local volume is $V_e(r) = |B_r(e)|$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Local neighborhood profile}

\begin{definition}[Neighborhood profile]\label{def:profile}
The \textit{neighborhood profile} of event $e$ is the sequence:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{V}(e) = (V_e(0), V_e(1), V_e(2), \ldots)
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\subsection{Flatness}

\begin{definition}[Local flatness]\label{def:flatness}
A subset $S \subset \Ecal$ is \textit{locally flat at radius $r$} if all events in $S$ have identical neighborhood profiles up to radius $r$:
\begin{equation}
    V_e(k) = V_{e'}(k) \quad \text{for all } e, e' \in S \text{ and all } k \leq r
\end{equation}
The event set $\Ecal$ is \textit{globally flat} if it is locally flat at radius $r$ for all $r \leq \mathrm{diam}(\Gcal)/2$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
Local flatness means that the transition graph has uniform volume-growth profiles from every event in $S$ out to radius $r$. This is a discrete analogue of local homogeneity in Riemannian geometry (though it is weaker than local graph isomorphism).
\end{remark}

%==================================================================
\section{Curvature Candidate A: Volume-Growth Distortion}\label{sec:curv_A}
%==================================================================

\begin{definition}[Volume-growth curvature]\label{def:Kgrow}
For an event $e$ at radius $r$, with a reference volume profile $V_{\mathrm{ref}}(r)$ (from a designated flat/symmetric region or the event-set average), define:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kgrow}
    K_{\mathrm{grow}}(e; r) = \frac{V_{\mathrm{ref}}(r) - V_e(r)}{V_{\mathrm{ref}}(r)}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
$K_{\mathrm{grow}} = 0$: event $e$ has the same local volume as the reference (flat). $K_{\mathrm{grow}} > 0$ indicates slower-than-reference local growth, while $K_{\mathrm{grow}} < 0$ indicates faster-than-reference local growth. These signs are heuristically analogous to positive and negative curvature, respectively, but no smooth-curvature identification is claimed here.
\end{remark}

%==================================================================
\section{Curvature Candidate B: Branching Asymmetry}\label{sec:curv_B}
%==================================================================

\begin{remark}
The directed Hasse graph (with edges $e_1 \to e_2$ when $e_1 \lessdot e_2$) also carries in-degree and out-degree information. The undirected definition above is preferred for the branching curvature indicator because it ensures the flat reference model has genuinely vanishing curvature. Directed branching asymmetry (in-degree vs out-degree) may serve as an additional causal-anisotropy indicator in future work.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Branching curvature]\label{def:Kbranch}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kbranch}
    K_{\mathrm{branch}}(e) = \left|\deg_{\Gcal}(e) - \overline{\deg}\right|
\end{equation}
where $\deg_{\Gcal}(e)$ is the degree of $e$ in the undirected transition graph and $\overline{\deg}$ is the mean degree over $\Ecal$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
$K_{\mathrm{branch}} = 0$: the event has exactly average connectivity (locally regular). $K_{\mathrm{branch}} > 0$: the event has anomalous connectivity --- either a bottleneck (much higher degree than average) or a peripheral event (much lower degree). This measures structural non-uniformity in event accessibility.
\end{remark}

%==================================================================
\section{Curvature Candidate C: Geodesic Concentration}\label{sec:curv_C}
%==================================================================

\begin{definition}[Geodesic betweenness]\label{def:between}
For event $e$, define the geodesic betweenness centrality:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:between}
    \beta(e) = \sum_{\substack{s,t \in \Ecal \\ s \neq e \neq t}} \frac{\sigma_{st}(e)}{\sigma_{st}}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{st}$ is the number of shortest paths from $s$ to $t$ in $\Gcal$, and $\sigma_{st}(e)$ is the number that pass through $e$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Geodesic-concentration curvature]\label{def:Kgeo}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kgeo}
    K_{\mathrm{geo}}(e) = \frac{\beta(e) - \bar{\beta}}{\bar{\beta}}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\beta}$ is the mean betweenness over $\Ecal$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
$K_{\mathrm{geo}} = 0$: the event carries its fair share of geodesics (uniform routing). $K_{\mathrm{geo}} > 0$: the event is a bottleneck --- geodesics concentrate through it. This is the free-fall analogue: paths through the event structure are drawn toward high-betweenness regions, just as geodesics in GR are drawn toward massive bodies.
\end{remark}

%==================================================================
\section{Geodesics and Free Fall}\label{sec:geodesic}
%==================================================================

\begin{definition}[Geodesic]\label{def:geodesic}
A \textit{geodesic} between events $e_1$ and $e_2$ is a shortest path in $(\Ecal, d_{\mathrm{trans}})$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
In a flat event geometry (all curvature indicators zero), geodesics are uniformly distributed: no event carries a disproportionate share of shortest paths. In a curved event geometry (nonzero curvature indicators), geodesics concentrate through specific regions.

This concentration is the structural analogue of gravitational free fall: objects (worldlines, chains) in the event structure are drawn toward regions of high accessibility, not by an imposed force, but by the geometry of the event graph itself. The ``force'' of gravity, in this picture, is the gradient of event accessibility.
\end{remark}

\section{Flat Toy Model: Symmetric Two-Layer}\label{sec:flat_toy}
%==================================================================

\begin{example}[Symmetric pentagon model]\label{ex:flat}
The dual-pentagon model from Papers~XXIII--XXV: 5 events in Layer~0, 5 in Layer~1, with every Layer-0 event covering every Layer-1 event. The Hasse graph is $K_{5,5}$ (complete bipartite).

\textbf{Neighborhood profiles.} Every Layer-0 event $e$ has: $V_e(0) = 1$, $V_e(1) = 6$ (itself + 5 Layer-1 events), $V_e(2) = 10$ (all events). By symmetry, every Layer-1 event has the same profile.

\textbf{Curvature indicators.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $K_{\mathrm{grow}}(e; 1) = 0$ for all $e$ (all events have the same ball size).
    \item $K_{\mathrm{branch}}(e) = 0$ for all $e$ (every vertex in $K_{5,5}$ has undirected degree 5; $\overline{\deg} = 5$).
    \item $K_{\mathrm{geo}}(e) = 0$ for all $e$ (by the vertex-transitive symmetry of $K_{5,5}$ with equal partition sizes, all vertices have equal betweenness).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Conclusion.} The symmetric two-layer model is flat: all curvature indicators vanish. This establishes the baseline.
\end{example}

\section{Curved Toy Model: Asymmetric Three-Layer with Bottleneck}\label{sec:curved_toy}
%==================================================================

\begin{example}[Bottleneck model]\label{ex:curved}
Introduce a three-layer event graph:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 0}: 4 events $\{a_1, a_2, a_3, a_4\}$.
    \item \textbf{Layer 1}: 1 event $\{b\}$ (the bottleneck).
    \item \textbf{Layer 2}: 4 events $\{c_1, c_2, c_3, c_4\}$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Cover relations:} $a_i \lessdot b$ for all $i$, and $b \lessdot c_j$ for all $j$. No other cover relations.

\textbf{Hasse graph:} A ``bowtie'' or hourglass --- all Layer-0 events connect to the single bottleneck, which connects to all Layer-2 events.

\textbf{Partial order:} $a_i \prec b \prec c_j$ for all $i, j$. Within each layer, events are incomparable.

\subsection*{Neighborhood profiles}

\begin{itemize}
    \item $a_i$: $V_{a_i}(0) = 1$, $V_{a_i}(1) = 2$ (itself + $b$), $V_{a_i}(2) = 6$ (itself + $b$ + 4 $c$-events).
    \item $b$: $V_b(0) = 1$, $V_b(1) = 9$ (itself + 4 $a$'s + 4 $c$'s), $V_b(2) = 9$ (all events).
    \item $c_j$: $V_{c_j}(0) = 1$, $V_{c_j}(1) = 2$ (itself + $b$), $V_{c_j}(2) = 6$.
\end{itemize}

The profiles differ: $V_b(1) = 9 \neq V_{a_i}(1) = 2$. \textbf{Not flat.}

\subsection*{Curvature indicators}

\textbf{Volume-growth curvature} (with $V_{\mathrm{ref}}(1) = \bar{V}(1) = (4 \times 2 + 1 \times 9 + 4 \times 2)/9 = 25/9 \approx 2.78$):
\begin{itemize}
    \item $K_{\mathrm{grow}}(a_i; 1) = (2.78 - 2)/2.78 = 0.28 > 0$ (smaller than average --- positive curvature).
    \item $K_{\mathrm{grow}}(b; 1) = (2.78 - 9)/2.78 = -2.24 < 0$ (larger than average --- negative curvature).
    \item $K_{\mathrm{grow}}(c_j; 1) = 0.28 > 0$.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Branching curvature.} Undirected degrees: $\deg(a_i) = 1$, $\deg(b) = 8$, $\deg(c_j) = 1$. Mean degree: $\overline{\deg} = (4 \times 1 + 1 \times 8 + 4 \times 1)/9 = 16/9 \approx 1.78$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item $K_{\mathrm{branch}}(a_i) = |1 - 16/9| = 7/9 \approx 0.78$.
    \item $K_{\mathrm{branch}}(b) = |8 - 16/9| = 56/9 \approx 6.2$ (highest).
    \item $K_{\mathrm{branch}}(c_j) = |1 - 16/9| = 7/9 \approx 0.78$.
\end{itemize}

The bottleneck $b$ has by far the highest branching curvature.

\textbf{Geodesic concentration.} Every shortest path between a Layer-0 event and a Layer-2 event passes through $b$. There are $4 \times 4 = 16$ such pairs, and $b$ lies on all 16 geodesics. Paths between same-layer events also pass through $b$ (e.g.\ $a_1 \to b \to a_2$ is not a geodesic since $a_1$ and $a_2$ are not adjacent; but $a_1 \to b \to c_1$ is a geodesic of length 2). Every shortest path between distinct non-bottleneck events passes through $b$, since there are no within-layer edges.

Betweenness: $\beta(b) \gg \beta(a_i) = \beta(c_j)$, so $K_{\mathrm{geo}}(b) \gg 0$.

\subsection*{Free-fall analogue}

All geodesics between distinct layers pass through the bottleneck $b$. This means that any ``motion'' through the event structure from Layer~0 to Layer~2 is drawn through $b$ --- not by an imposed force, but by the geometry of accessibility. The bottleneck acts as a gravitational attractor in the event graph.

\textbf{Conclusion.} The three-layer bottleneck model is curved: all curvature indicators are nonzero, and geodesics concentrate through the bottleneck. This demonstrates that non-uniform event accessibility produces detectable geometric distortion.
\end{example}

\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:discussion}
%==================================================================

\subsection{What is established}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Flatness defined as local neighborhood-profile uniformity (Definition~\ref{def:flatness}).
    \item Three curvature indicators constructed: volume-growth, branching, geodesic concentration (Definitions~\ref{def:Kgrow}, \ref{def:Kbranch}, \ref{def:Kgeo}).
    \item Flat toy model: all indicators vanish (Example~\ref{ex:flat}).
    \item Curved toy model: all indicators nonzero, bottleneck concentrates geodesics (Example~\ref{ex:curved}).
    \item Free-fall analogue: geodesics drawn through high-accessibility regions (Section~\ref{sec:geodesic}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What is not established}

\begin{itemize}
    \item No Einstein equations. The relationship between curvature and matter/energy content is not derived.
    \item No curvature tensor. The indicators are scalar quantities, not tensorial.
    \item No continuous limit. Whether the discrete curvature converges to smooth Riemann/Ricci curvature is open.
    \item No dynamics. How curvature evolves or responds to event-structure changes is not addressed.
    \item The curvature indicators are defined relative to a reference profile; the choice of reference may affect the values.
    \item The toy models are illustrative; the full 600-cell event structure is not analysed.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Programme position}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Paper~XXIII: event ordering (derived).
    \item Paper~XXIV: observer kinematics (derived).
    \item Paper~XXV: separation geometry (derived).
    \item Paper~XXVI (this paper): curvature precursor (constructed).
    \item Future: dynamics / field equations (how matter sources curvature).
\end{itemize}

%==================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%==================================================================

The event-order framework of Papers~XXIII--XXV naturally supports a curvature precursor. Flatness is defined as local uniformity of the transition-graph neighborhood structure; curvature is defined as deviation from that uniformity. Three indicators --- volume-growth distortion, branching asymmetry, and geodesic concentration --- provide complementary measures of local geometric distortion.

The flat toy model (symmetric $K_{5,5}$) has vanishing curvature. The curved toy model (asymmetric bottleneck) has nonzero curvature, with geodesics concentrating through the bottleneck in a structural analogue of gravitational free fall: geodesic propagation through the event structure follows the geometry of accessibility without any imposed force.

The GR programme now carries: temporal ordering, observer-dependent kinematics, separation geometry, and curvature. What remains is the connection between event-structure distortion and matter/energy content --- the route to dynamical field equations.
