\begin{abstract}
Papers~XXIII--XXIV derived a strict partial order on admissible overlap events and showed that observer-dependent time functions, relativity of simultaneity, and time-dilation analogues emerge from the event poset. The present paper addresses the next question: what does it mean for two events to be \textit{near or far}?

Three candidate separation constructions are developed and compared. For comparable events (those related by the partial order), a \textit{chain-length separation} counts the minimum number of cover steps between them, providing an observer-independent temporal interval. For incomparable events (those not causally related), an \textit{observer-disagreement separation} measures the variance of time-difference assignments across observers, providing a structural precursor to spatial distance. A \textit{transition-cost separation} on the Hasse graph gives a unified graph-distance that applies to both cases.

The chain-length separation is proved to be a well-defined directed temporal separation on comparable pairs. The observer-disagreement construction is shown to exhibit observer-independent sign for comparable pairs and observer-dependent sign for incomparable pairs, with the variance quantity providing a nontrivial separation measure. When the transition graph is connected, the transition-cost construction satisfies all metric axioms on the event set. A worked example on the dual-pentagon system demonstrates all three constructions explicitly.

The paper does not derive the Lorentz metric or continuous geometry. It establishes that the event-order framework supports a nontrivial separation structure, providing a concrete route from poset to emergent metric geometry.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%==================================================================

Paper~XXIII~\citep{SmartXXIII} derived a strict partial order $\prec$ on an event set $\Ecal$ from phase-overlap geometry. Paper~XXIV~\citep{SmartXXIV} showed that observers (linear extensions with sampling measures) produce frame-dependent time functions, relativity of simultaneity, and time-dilation analogues.

These results give the event set temporal and observer structure, but not spatial or metric structure. The present paper asks: \textit{given only the event poset and its observer completions, can one define a meaningful notion of separation between events?}

\subsection*{The essential distinction}

Two events can be related in exactly two ways under $\prec$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Comparable} ($e_1 \prec e_2$ or $e_2 \prec e_1$): causally / temporally ordered.
    \item \textbf{Incomparable} (neither holds): ``simultaneous'' / spacelike-separated.
\end{itemize}

Any separation construction must handle both cases, and the two cases may require different structures. The paper develops three candidates and compares them.

\subsection*{Scope}

This paper constructs separation quantities and proves their basic properties. It does not derive the Lorentz metric, continuous manifold structure, or the Einstein equations. It establishes that the event-order framework naturally supports emergent metric-like geometry.

\section{Cover Relations and the Transition Graph}\label{sec:hasse}
%==================================================================

\begin{definition}[Cover relation]\label{def:cover}
Event $e_1$ is \textit{covered by} $e_2$ (written $e_1 \lessdot e_2$) if $e_1 \prec e_2$ and there is no event $e$ with $e_1 \prec e \prec e_2$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Transition graph (Hasse graph)]\label{def:hasse}
The \textit{transition graph} $\Gcal = (\Ecal, E_H)$ has vertex set $\Ecal$ and edge set
\begin{equation}
    E_H = \{(e_1, e_2) : e_1 \lessdot e_2\}
\end{equation}
This is the Hasse diagram of $(\Ecal, \prec)$, viewed as an undirected graph (ignoring edge orientation for the purpose of graph distance).
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The cover relation encodes ``immediate succession'': $e_1 \lessdot e_2$ means $e_2$ is the next event after $e_1$ with no intervening event in the partial order. The transition graph captures the minimal-step accessibility structure of the event set.
\end{remark}

\section{Construction A: Chain-Length Separation}\label{sec:chain}
%==================================================================

For comparable events, the natural separation is the number of order steps between them.

\begin{definition}[Chain-length separation]\label{def:dchain}
For comparable events $e_1 \prec e_2$, define:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dchain}
    d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2) = \min\{|C| - 1 : C \text{ is a chain from } e_1 \text{ to } e_2\}
\end{equation}
where $|C|$ is the number of events in $C$. For $e_1 = e_2$, set $d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e, e) = 0$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
$d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2) = k$ means there is a chain $e_1 = c_0 \prec c_1 \prec \cdots \prec c_k = e_2$ of length $k$, and no shorter chain exists. This is the minimum number of ``causal steps'' between $e_1$ and $e_2$.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Properties of chain-length separation]\label{prop:dchain}
On the set of comparable pairs, $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ satisfies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Non-negativity:} $d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2) \geq 0$.
    \item \textbf{Identity:} $d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e, e) = 0$.
    \item \textbf{Triangle inequality:} If $e_1 \prec e_2 \prec e_3$:
    \begin{equation}
        d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_3) \leq d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2) + d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_2, e_3)
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
(1): Chain lengths are non-negative integers. (2): The trivial chain $\{e\}$ has $|C| - 1 = 0$. (3): Concatenating a shortest chain from $e_1$ to $e_2$ with one from $e_2$ to $e_3$ gives a chain from $e_1$ to $e_3$ of length $d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2) + d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_2, e_3)$. The minimum over all chains can only be shorter.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
$d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ is a directed separation function, not a metric: it is only defined for comparable pairs and is inherently directional (from earlier to later). Incomparable events have no chain between them, so $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ does not apply. This separation is \textit{temporal}: it measures causal distance, not spatial distance.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Longest chain as proper-time analogue]
In causal set theory, the longest (not shortest) chain between comparable events is the analogue of proper time. Define:
\begin{equation}
    d_{\max}(e_1, e_2) = \max\{|C| - 1 : C \text{ is a chain from } e_1 \text{ to } e_2\}
\end{equation}
$d_{\max}$ is well-defined for comparable pairs but does not in general satisfy a metric-style triangle inequality. It is better viewed as an alternative temporal-size functional. In the VFD framework, whether $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ (shortest) or $d_{\max}$ (longest) better approximates physical proper time is an open question; in causal set theory, the longest chain is preferred.
\end{remark}

\section{Construction B: Observer-Disagreement Separation}\label{sec:disagreement}
%==================================================================

For incomparable events, no chain exists and $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ is undefined. A different construction is needed.

\begin{definition}[Observer-disagreement separation]\label{def:dobs}
For any two events $e_1, e_2 \in \Ecal$, let $\Ocal$ be a family of observers. Define:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dobs}
    \sigma(e_1, e_2) = \mathrm{Var}_{O \in \Ocal}\!\left[t_O(e_1) - t_O(e_2)\right]
\end{equation}
the variance of the time-difference assignment across the observer family.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Observer-dependent sign structure]\label{prop:dobs}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item If $e_1 = e_2$: $t_O(e_1) - t_O(e_2) = 0$ for all observers $O$.
    \item If $e_1 \prec e_2$ (comparable): $t_O(e_1) - t_O(e_2) < 0$ for all observers $O$. The sign is observer-independent.
    \item If $e_1$ and $e_2$ are incomparable: there exist observers $O_1, O_2$ such that
    \[
        t_{O_1}(e_1) - t_{O_1}(e_2) < 0, \qquad t_{O_2}(e_1) - t_{O_2}(e_2) > 0.
    \]
    The sign is observer-dependent.
\end{enumerate}
Consequently, the variance $\sigma(e_1, e_2)$ is zero for identical events, and generically nonzero for incomparable pairs (where the sign-changing contributions ensure positive variance).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
(1): Immediate. (2): $e_1 \prec e_2$ implies $t_O(e_1) < t_O(e_2)$ for all $O$ (Proposition~1 of Paper~XXIV). (3): By the relativity-of-simultaneity theorem (Theorem~1 of Paper~XXIV), linear extensions exist placing the pair in either order.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation]
The observer-disagreement $\sigma$ serves as a \textit{spatial-separation precursor}: it is large for events that observers strongly disagree about, and zero only for identical events. Comparable events have constrained (sign-fixed) disagreement; incomparable events admit sign-varying disagreement across observers. This mirrors the physical intuition that timelike-separated events have a fixed ordering while spacelike-separated events do not.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Metric failure]
$\sigma$ does not satisfy the triangle inequality in general and is therefore not a metric. It is a meaningful \textit{separation measure} but not a distance function in the strict sense. Its role is to provide nontrivial structure for incomparable pairs where $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ is undefined.
\end{remark}

\section{Construction C: Transition-Cost Metric}\label{sec:transition}
%==================================================================

The transition graph $\Gcal$ (Definition~\ref{def:hasse}) provides a unified metric that applies to both comparable and incomparable events.

\begin{definition}[Transition-cost metric]\label{def:dtrans}
For any $e_1, e_2 \in \Ecal$, define:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dtrans}
    d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_1, e_2) = \text{shortest-path length in } \Gcal \text{ between } e_1 \text{ and } e_2
\end{equation}
where the graph $\Gcal$ is the undirected Hasse graph with unit edge weights.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Metric on $\Ecal$]\label{thm:metric}
If $\Gcal$ is connected, then $d_{\mathrm{trans}}$ is a metric on $\Ecal$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_1, e_2) \geq 0$, with equality iff $e_1 = e_2$.
    \item $d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_1, e_2) = d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_2, e_1)$.
    \item $d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_1, e_3) \leq d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_1, e_2) + d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_2, e_3)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
These are the standard properties of shortest-path distance on a connected graph. (1): Shortest paths have non-negative length; length 0 iff $e_1 = e_2$. (2): Undirected graph, so paths are reversible. (3): Triangle inequality holds for shortest-path distance on any graph (concatenation gives an upper bound).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
$d_{\mathrm{trans}}$ is the first genuine metric on the event set. Unlike $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ (which requires comparability) and $\sigma$ (which fails the triangle inequality), $d_{\mathrm{trans}}$ applies to all pairs and satisfies all metric axioms.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Comparable vs incomparable]
For comparable events $e_1 \prec e_2$, the transition-cost distance satisfies $d_{\mathrm{trans}}(e_1, e_2) \leq d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2)$ (since any chain gives a path in the Hasse graph). In the pentagon toy model, equality holds: $d_{\mathrm{trans}} = d_{\mathrm{chain}} = 1$ for all comparable pairs. Whether equality holds in general depends on the specific Hasse-graph structure. For incomparable events, $d_{\mathrm{trans}}$ involves ``sideways'' steps through the graph --- paths that go up then down (or vice versa) through the partial order. These sideways paths are the structural precursor to spatial separation.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Analogy to Lorentzian structure]
In Lorentzian geometry, timelike intervals are computed along causal curves, and spacelike intervals require leaving the light cone. The VFD framework exhibits a structural analogue: comparable events have chain-based (causal-curve) separation, while incomparable events require sideways (off-chain) paths in the transition graph. The transition-cost metric $d_{\mathrm{trans}}$ does not distinguish timelike from spacelike --- it is a single positive-definite distance. A signed interval $s^2 = d_{\mathrm{chain}}^2 - d_{\mathrm{sideways}}^2$ could in principle be defined but is not pursued here.
\end{remark}

\section{Observer Time vs Structural Interval}\label{sec:comparison}
%==================================================================

\begin{proposition}[Structural bound on observer time]\label{prop:bound}
For comparable events $e_1 \prec e_2$ and any observer $O = (\leq_O, \mu_O)$:
\begin{equation}
    t_O(e_2) - t_O(e_1) \geq d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2) \cdot \min_{e \in \Ecal} \mu_O(e)
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $e_1 = c_0 \prec c_1 \prec \cdots \prec c_k = e_2$ be a shortest chain ($k = d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e_1, e_2)$). Since $\leq_O$ extends $\prec$, the observer ordering satisfies $c_0 \leq_O c_1 \leq_O \cdots \leq_O c_k$. Therefore all $k$ events $c_1, \ldots, c_k$ lie in the interval $\{e' : e_1 <_O e' \leq_O e_2\}$. Each contributes at least $\min_{e} \mu_O(e)$ to the sum.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This proposition connects observer-dependent time to observer-independent structure: the chain-length separation provides a lower bound on any observer's elapsed time, scaled by the observer's minimum sampling rate. The structural interval ``lives underneath'' all observer times.
\end{remark}

\section{Worked Example: Dual Pentagons}\label{sec:example}
%==================================================================

\begin{example}[Pentagon separation structure]\label{ex:pentagon}
Using the dual-pentagon event set from Papers~XXIII--XXIV: 10 events in two layers.

\textbf{Layer 0} ($\theta_b = 0$): events $(0,0), (1,1), (2,2), (3,3), (4,4)$.

\textbf{Layer 1} ($\theta_b = 2\pi/5$): events $(0,4), (1,0), (2,1), (3,2), (4,3)$.

\textbf{Partial order:} Every Layer-0 event precedes every Layer-1 event. Events within the same layer are incomparable.

\textbf{Cover relations:} Every Layer-0 event covers every Layer-1 event (since there are no intermediate events). So $e \lessdot f$ for all $e$ in Layer~0 and $f$ in Layer~1.

\textbf{Hasse graph:} Complete bipartite graph $K_{5,5}$ between the two layers.

\subsection*{Chain-length separation (comparable pairs)}

For any Layer-0 event $e$ and Layer-1 event $f$: $d_{\mathrm{chain}}(e, f) = 1$ (one cover step). All comparable pairs have the same chain-length separation.

\subsection*{Observer-disagreement separation (incomparable pairs)}

Within Layer~0, events $(0,0)$ and $(1,1)$ are incomparable. Using two observers from Paper~XXIV:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $O_1$: assigns $t_{O_1}(0,0) = 1$, $t_{O_1}(1,1) = 2$. Difference: $-1$.
    \item $O_2$: assigns $t_{O_2}(0,0) = 10$, $t_{O_2}(1,1) = 6$. Difference: $+4$.
\end{itemize}
Mean difference: $(-1 + 4)/2 = 1.5$. Variance: $\sigma = ((−1−1.5)^2 + (4−1.5)^2)/2 = (6.25 + 6.25)/2 = 6.25$.

So $\sigma((0,0),(1,1)) = 6.25 > 0$: the incomparable events are distinguished by observer disagreement.

\subsection*{Transition-cost metric (all pairs)}

The Hasse graph is $K_{5,5}$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Comparable pair (different layers): $d_{\mathrm{trans}} = 1$ (direct edge).
    \item Incomparable pair (same layer): $d_{\mathrm{trans}} = 2$ (go to any event in the other layer, then back). For example, $(0,0) \to (0,4) \to (1,1)$: path length 2.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Summary}

\begin{table}[ht]
\centering
\caption{Separations in the pentagon toy model.}
\label{tab:pentagon}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
Pair type & $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ & $\sigma$ & $d_{\mathrm{trans}}$ \\
\midrule
Same event & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
Comparable (cross-layer) & 1 & $> 0$ (fixed sign) & 1 \\
Incomparable (same layer) & undefined & $6.25$ (sign-varying) & 2 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The transition-cost metric gives $d_{\mathrm{trans}} = 1$ for ``timelike'' (comparable) pairs and $d_{\mathrm{trans}} = 2$ for ``spacelike'' (incomparable) pairs. Incomparable events are \textit{farther} in the transition metric than comparable ones --- the sideways cost exceeds the forward cost.
\end{example}

\section{Discussion and Limitations}\label{sec:discussion}
%==================================================================

\subsection{What is established}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Cover relation and Hasse graph explicitly defined (Definitions~\ref{def:cover}--\ref{def:hasse}).
    \item Chain-length separation: well-defined directed temporal separation on comparable pairs (Proposition~\ref{prop:dchain}).
    \item Observer-sign structure: comparable pairs have observer-independent sign; incomparable pairs have observer-dependent sign (Proposition~\ref{prop:dobs}). Variance $\sigma$ provides a quantitative separation measure but does not satisfy the triangle inequality (explicitly noted).
    \item Transition-cost metric: a genuine metric on the full event set (Theorem~\ref{thm:metric}).
    \item Structural bound relating observer time to chain length (Proposition~\ref{prop:bound}).
    \item All constructions demonstrated on the pentagon toy model.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What is not established}

\begin{itemize}
    \item No Lorentzian metric. The transition-cost metric is positive-definite, not Lorentzian.
    \item No signed interval. A $ds^2 = dt^2 - dx^2$ decomposition is not derived.
    \item No continuous manifold. All constructions are discrete (graph-based).
    \item No Einstein equations or curvature. The metric is flat in the toy model.
    \item The observer-disagreement quantity is not a metric (triangle inequality failure is explicit).
    \item The Hasse graph connectivity (required for Theorem~\ref{thm:metric}) is assumed, not proved for the full 600-cell event structure.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Metric failure modes (honest assessment)}

\begin{itemize}
    \item $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$: only defined for comparable pairs; it is a directed temporal separation, not a metric on full $\Ecal$.
    \item $\sigma$: fails triangle inequality; not a metric in the formal sense.
    \item $d_{\mathrm{trans}}$: satisfies all axioms but is positive-definite, losing the timelike/spacelike distinction that Lorentzian geometry requires.
\end{itemize}

A future construction that recovers a signed interval from the interplay of $d_{\mathrm{chain}}$ and $d_{\mathrm{trans}}$ would represent a significant step toward Lorentzian structure. The present paper provides the ingredients.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%==================================================================

The event-order framework of Papers~XXIII--XXIV naturally supports emergent separation structure. Three constructions are developed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Chain-length separation}: an observer-independent directed temporal separation for comparable events (properties proved).
    \item \textbf{Observer-disagreement separation}: a nontrivial observer-dependent separation measure for incomparable events, interpreted as a spatial precursor (sign structure proved; not a metric).
    \item \textbf{Transition-cost metric}: a genuine metric on the full event set from Hasse-graph shortest paths (metric axioms proved, conditional on graph connectivity).
\end{itemize}

The structural distinction between comparable (chain-based, temporal) and incomparable (sideways, spatial-precursor) separation mirrors the timelike/spacelike distinction of Lorentzian geometry, though a signed interval is not yet derived.

The event-order framework now carries: temporal ordering (Paper~XXIII), observer-dependent kinematics (Paper~XXIV), and separation geometry (this paper). What remains for the GR programme is the construction of curvature and dynamical equations on this structure.
